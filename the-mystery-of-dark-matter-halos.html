The Mystery of Dark Matter Halos

New simulations reveal how dark matter shapes the cosmic web and influences galaxy formation across billions of years.

Dark matter, an elusive substance that doesn't interact with light, is believed to make up about 27% of the universe. Its gravitational influence is evident in the rotation of galaxies and the dynamics of galaxy clusters, yet it remains undetected directly. Scientists theorize that galaxies are embedded within vast 'halos' of dark matter, which provide the gravitational scaffolding for visible matter.

Recent high-resolution simulations, leveraging supercomputing power, have allowed astrophysicists to model the evolution of these dark matter halos from the early universe to the present day. These simulations track billions of dark matter particles as they coalesce and grow under gravity, forming intricate cosmic webs.

The findings indicate that the distribution of dark matter is far more complex than previously imagined, with a hierarchical structure of halos and sub-halos influencing the formation and mergers of galaxies. Understanding these structures is crucial for refining our cosmological models and unraveling the mysteries of galaxy evolution.

Further research involves comparing these simulations with observational data from telescopes, looking for gravitational lensing effects and other indirect evidence of dark matter distribution. As technology advances, new instruments may provide direct clues about the nature of dark matter itself.
